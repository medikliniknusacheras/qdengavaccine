THREATENING FROM DENGUE

Dengue is a significant concern in Malaysia due to its high incidence and prevalence, with the virus being endemic in the country and recurring outbreaks causing substantial health challenges. The disease leads to severe health impacts, including high morbidity and mortality rates, especially among vulnerable populations like children, the elderly, and those with underlying health conditions. Severe dengue, known as Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever or Dengue Shock Syndrome, can be fatal if not promptly treated, resulting in complications such as plasma leakage, severe bleeding, and organ impairment.

The economic burden of dengue is considerable, with substantial healthcare costs for hospitalization, treatment, and supportive care, alongside the loss of productivity due to illness and hospitalization. Malaysia's climate and environment further exacerbate the dengue threat. The presence of Aedes aegypti and Aedes albopictus mosquitoes, the primary vectors for dengue, is widespread due to the warm and humid climate, which is ideal for mosquito breeding. Rapid urbanization and population growth increase human-mosquito interactions, especially in densely populated areas with inadequate water storage and waste management practices.

Dengue is also a global health concern, recognized by organizations such as the World Health Organization (WHO), and poses risks to international travelers, potentially spreading the virus beyond Malaysia's borders. Effective public health measures are crucial to address this threat, including vector control programs, public awareness campaigns, and enhanced surveillance and monitoring systems. Continuous efforts in these areas are essential to mitigate the impact of dengue and protect public health in Malaysia.

Dengue Statistics in Malaysia

Dengue Cases and Trends

In 2023, Malaysia reported a significant number of dengue cases, reaching over 130,000 cases with approximately 200 deaths. The trend over the past decade shows fluctuating numbers with peak years coinciding with high rainfall and ideal breeding conditions for mosquitoes. Urban areas such as Kuala Lumpur, Selangor, and Penang report the highest number of cases due to higher population density and environmental conditions conducive to mosquito breeding.

Age and Gender Distribution

Dengue affects all age groups, but the highest incidence rates are found in children aged 5-14 years. Males are slightly more affected than females, with a ratio of approximately 1.2:1.

Seasonal Variation

Dengue cases typically surge during the rainy season from May to September. The increased rainfall creates more breeding sites for Aedes mosquitoes, leading to higher transmission rates.

Introduction to Qdenga Vaccine

MOH Policies

The Ministry of Health (MOH) Malaysia has approved the use of the Qdenga dengue vaccine as part of the national immunization program. Policies include vaccination campaigns in high-risk areas and targeted immunization for vulnerable populations.

Efficacy

Clinical trials have shown that the Qdenga vaccine is approximately 80% effective in preventing symptomatic dengue infections. The vaccine also significantly reduces the severity of the disease in those who contract dengue post-vaccination.

Indications and Criteria

The Qdenga vaccine is recommended for individuals aged 9-45 years living in dengue-endemic areas. It is particularly indicated for those with a history of previous dengue infection, as it provides enhanced immunity.
